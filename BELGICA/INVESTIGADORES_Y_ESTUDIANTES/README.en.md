# INTERNATIONAL RESEARCHERS AND STUDENTS IN BELGIUM

> People who migrate for academic reasons face a particular combination of vulnerabilities: residency status tied to their employer or institution, social isolation, performance pressure, and language barriers to accessing help. Studies show that PhD students are 6 times more likely to experience anxiety and depression than the general population *(Nature Biotechnology, 2018)*. This section exists so you know your rights before you need them.


## WORK PERMITS: WHO IS RESPONSIBLE FOR WHAT

### The Single Permit

The Single Permit (*Titre de Séjour Unique*) is the document combining residence and work authorisation for non-EU nationals working in Belgium for more than 90 days.

**The responsibility for applying lies with the employer, not the worker.**

This includes research contracts, MSCA projects, and all employment contracts. The employer must:
- Submit the application to the competent regional authority
- Do so ideally at least **60 days before** the contract start date
- Manage the renewal **before** the current permit expires

> If your employer tells you that renewing your permit is "your responsibility", that is incorrect. You can point this out based on Belgian law and, if they do not resolve it, contact a trade union or UNIA.

**Processing times:**
- New application: up to **60 days** from the moment the file is complete
- If documents are missing: 15 days to complete the file

**More information:**
- IBZ (Immigration Office): https://dofi.ibz.be/en/themes/ressortissants-dun-pays-tiers/travail/researchers
- EU Immigration Portal: https://home-affairs.ec.europa.eu/policies/migration-and-asylum/eu-immigration-portal/researcher-belgium_en

### The Orientation Year

If you graduated from a Belgian institution and want to stay to look for work or start a contract, you can apply for the Orientation Year — a one-year extension of your student permit.

To prove financial capacity, the options are:
- Having a financial sponsor
- Having part-time employment (even informal work)
- Blocking **€11,500** at your graduating university

> If you already have a job offer or signed contract, your employer should be able to provide an acceptance letter for the commune procedure. If they refuse without clear reason, consult a trade union or MYRIA.

- More information: https://ctrl-f.be/en/blog/candidates/the-search-year-for-non-eu-students-everything-you-need-to-know/


## MSCA: YOUR RIGHTS AS A MARIE CURIE RESEARCHER

Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions (MSCA) projects must comply with the **European Charter for Researchers** and the **Code of Conduct for the Recruitment of Researchers**, which establish:

- Decent and non-discriminatory working conditions
- Gender balance and protection against discrimination based on origin, gender, sexual orientation, or other characteristics
- Intellectual property rights
- Adequate supervision and continuous training

If your employer or supervisor fails to meet these conditions, you have the right to contact the **Research Executive Agency (REA)** of the European Commission directly, which manages MSCA projects:

- Website: https://rea.ec.europa.eu/funding-and-grants/horizon-europe-marie-sklodowska-curie-actions_en
- MSCA Alumni Association (peer support network): https://www.mariecuriealumni.eu

> If your employer or supervisor asks you not to contact MSCA about an irregularity, that in itself may be a warning sign. You have the right — and possibly the contractual obligation — to report abnormal situations.


## TRADE UNIONS: FREE LEGAL HELP FOR WORKERS

Trade unions in Belgium offer free legal defence to their members. Any worker can join, regardless of immigration status or nationality. Even without being a member, you can approach them to explain your situation.

The three recognised trade unions in Belgium are:

**FGTB / ABVV** (socialist)
- Address: Place Rouppe 3, 1000 Brussels
- Website: https://www.fgtb.be
- "Refugees and Work" project: free legal advice for migrant workers

**CSC / ACV** (Christian)
- Website: https://www.acv-csc.be
- Specific services for workers with irregular immigration status and for women in domestic care sectors
- Rights brochure in multiple languages: https://www.hetacv.be/docs/default-source/acv-csc-docsitemap/9000-militant/9150-publicaties-voor-militanten-brochures-pour-militants/91501-anderstalig/acv-brochure-belgium-trade-union-wise.pdf

**CGSLB / ACLVB** (liberal)
- Website: https://www.cgslb.be

> Trade unions operate primarily in Dutch and French. If you need support in Spanish, ask explicitly when you contact them — or bring someone you trust. Organisations like CIRÉ or CAW Brussel (see [Legal Aid](../ABOGADOS/README.en.md) section) can also accompany you in these processes.


## DISCRIMINATION: HOW TO REPORT IT

### UNIA — Interfederal Centre for Equal Opportunities

UNIA handles cases of discrimination based on racial or ethnic origin, nationality, skin colour, age, sexual orientation, disability, religious or philosophical beliefs, and other grounds. The service is **free and confidential**.

- Free phone: **0800 12 800** (workdays, 9:30 – 13:00)
- General phone: +32 (0)2 212 30 00
- Online form: https://www.melding.unia.be/en/report-it
- Address: Place Victor Horta 40, bte 40, 1060 Saint-Gilles, Brussels
- Website: https://www.unia.be/en/report-discrimination

**UNIA covers discrimination in:**
- The workplace (comments, differential treatment, dismissal)
- Educational and academic institutions
- Commercial activities, banking, public transport

> You can report discriminatory comments from supervisors, professors, or colleagues. You do not need to have been dismissed or suffered material consequences for your case to be heard. Comments such as "foreigners perform worse" or "you don't belong here" are discriminatory and reportable.

**Note:** UNIA does not cover gender-based discrimination (for that, see the [Institute for the Equality of Women and Men](https://igvm-iefh.belgium.be/en)).

## INSTITUTIONAL VIOLENCE: CONTEXT AND FURTHER READING

Institutional violence is not always intentional or visible. It can manifest as bureaucratic indifference, shifting responsibility onto the most vulnerable, or comments that normalise exclusion. This disproportionately affects migrants, women, racialised people, and Indigenous people in academic and professional environments.

**Reference readings:**
- Leguil, C. (2003). *Violence de l'institution.* Études, 399. https://www.em-consulte.com/article/19491/violence-de-l-institution *(in French)*

- Bourdieu, P. (1989). *La noblesse d'État.* Minuit. *(on the reproduction of inequalities in academic institutions)*
